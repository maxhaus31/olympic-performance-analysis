## Overview
This project analyzes factors contributing to Olympic success by examining more than 120 years of historical Olympic data enriched with external socioeconomic and geographic datasets. The goal was to identify the structural patterns behind medal success and demonstrate how data visualization can communicate complex analytical insights.
My particular focus was on exploring the correlation between performance and whether the Olympic Games took place in the home country or on the home continent, as well as the correlation between performance and distance traveled.

## Data Curation & Preparation
The primary dataset used was *‚Äú120 Years of Olympic History ‚Äì Athletes and Results‚Äù* (Kaggle), containing athlete-level records from 1896 to 2016.

Key data preparation steps included:
- Restricting the analysis to **Summer Olympic Games** to ensure data consistency and historical depth
- Handling missing medal values by explicitly labeling non-medal outcomes
- Creating derived features such as:
  - Binary medal indicators
  - Total medals per athlete
- Filtering out irregular or discontinued sports to reduce noise and ensure meaningful comparisons
- Integrating external datasets (e.g., Human Development Index) for socioeconomic analysis

The curated dataset formed the foundation for all subsequent analysis and visualizations.

## Key Insights
- Olympic success is strongly correlated with national development indicators such as HDI
- Medal-winning athletes differ physiologically from the general population in sport-specific ways, not through a universal ‚ÄúOlympic phenotype‚Äù
- Host nations experience a significant performance boost (~40%)
- Medal efficiency decreases as travel distance to the host city increases

These findings suggest that Olympic success is influenced by infrastructure, strategic investment, and geographic context, not only individual talent.
